ATEX Equipment Categories 1, 2, 3: Selection Guide for Hazardous Areas

Last updated: March 2026 · Based on ATEX 2014/34/EU, IEC 60079-0, and EN 13463-1

Quick Answer

ATEX equipment categories define the level of protection a piece of equipment provides against becoming an ignition source in an explosive atmosphere. Category 1 provides the highest level (suitable for Zone 0/20), Category 2 provides a high level (suitable for Zone 1/21), and Category 3 provides a normal level (suitable for Zone 2/22). Categories are defined in ATEX Directive 2014/34/EU and directly correspond to Equipment Protection Levels (EPL) in the IEC system.

Equipment Groups

Before understanding categories, you need to understand equipment groups. ATEX divides equipment into two main groups based on the intended environment:

Group I — Mining

Equipment intended for use in underground mines and surface installations of mines liable to firedamp (methane) and/or combustible dust. Group I equipment faces unique challenges: methane is always present in coal mines, ventilation can be interrupted, and equipment may be subject to mechanical damage from mining operations.

Group II — Surface Industries

Equipment intended for use in all other places where explosive atmospheres may occur. This covers the vast majority of applications: oil & gas, chemical processing, pharmaceuticals, food production, woodworking, paint spraying, and any other industry with flammable gases, vapors, or combustible dusts.

Group II is further subdivided by gas group for electrical equipment:

  • IIA: Least dangerous gases (propane, methane, acetone) — see gas groups
  • IIB: Intermediate gases (ethylene, diethyl ether)
  • IIC: Most dangerous gases (hydrogen, acetylene, carbon disulfide)

The Three Equipment Categories (Group II)

For Group II (surface industries), ATEX defines three equipment categories. The category number indicates the intended zone and the level of protection required:

Category 1 — Very High Level of Protection

Equipment suitable for use in Zone 0 (gas) or Zone 20 (dust) — areas where an explosive atmosphere is present continuously, for long periods, or frequently.

  • Design requirement: Equipment must remain safe with two independent faults
  • Fault tolerance: Two countable faults — the equipment continues to provide protection even if two independent protection features fail simultaneously
  • Equivalent EPL: Ga (gas) or Da (dust)
  • Protection methods: Only Ex ia (intrinsic safety level "a") and Ex ma (encapsulation level "a") qualify for Zone 0 gas. For Zone 20 dust: Ex ia, Ex ma, and Ex ta (protection by enclosure level "a")
  • Conformity assessment: Module B (EU Type Examination) + Module D (Quality assurance of production) or Module F (Product verification). A Notified Body must be involved in both design assessment and production surveillance.
  • Cost: Highest — typically 2–5× the cost of equivalent Category 3 equipment

Category 2 — High Level of Protection

Equipment suitable for use in Zone 1 (gas) or Zone 21 (dust) — areas where an explosive atmosphere is likely to occur during normal operation.

  • Design requirement: Equipment must remain safe with one countable fault
  • Fault tolerance: One countable fault — protection remains effective even with a single fault condition
  • Equivalent EPL: Gb (gas) or Db (dust)
  • Protection methods: Ex d (flameproof), Ex e (increased safety), Ex ib (intrinsic safety level "b"), Ex p (pressurization), Ex m (encapsulation), Ex o (oil immersion), Ex q (powder filling), and others with "b" suffix
  • Conformity assessment: Module B + Module D, E, or F. A Notified Body must perform the EU Type Examination.
  • Cost: Moderate — the most common category for industrial applications

Category 2 equipment is the workhorse of industrial explosion protection. The majority of Ex-certified equipment installed worldwide falls into this category.

Category 3 — Normal Level of Protection

Equipment suitable for use in Zone 2 (gas) or Zone 22 (dust) — areas where an explosive atmosphere is not likely during normal operation and will only occur infrequently and for short periods.

  • Design requirement: Equipment must not become an ignition source during normal operation
  • Fault tolerance: None — protection is not required under fault conditions (faults are expected to remove the equipment from service before ignition occurs)
  • Equivalent EPL: Gc (gas) or Dc (dust)
  • Protection methods: Ex nA (non-sparking), Ex nC (sealed/enclosed break), Ex nR (restricted breathing), Ex ic (intrinsic safety level "c"), Ex tc (protection by enclosure level "c")
  • Conformity assessment: Module A (internal production control) — the manufacturer can self-certify. No Notified Body involvement is required (though many manufacturers still use one).
  • Cost: Lowest — often only slightly more expensive than standard industrial equipment

Mining Categories (Group I)

Group I equipment for mines uses a different category structure with only two levels:

M1 — Very High Level of Protection (Mines)

  • Equipment can remain energized in an explosive atmosphere
  • Must remain safe with two independent faults
  • Equivalent EPL: Ma
  • Protection methods: Ex ia, Ex ma
  • Used for critical safety equipment that must keep functioning during a methane event (gas detectors, emergency communications, ventilation monitoring)

M2 — High Level of Protection (Mines)

  • Equipment must be de-energized when an explosive atmosphere is detected
  • Must remain safe with one fault
  • Equivalent EPL: Mb
  • Protection methods: Ex d, Ex e, Ex ib, Ex p, and others
  • Used for equipment that can be safely disconnected by automatic methane detection/shutdown systems (conveyors, lighting, power distribution)
Group Category EPL Zone Suitability Fault Tolerance Conformity Module
I (Mining) M1 Ma Remains energized in firedamp Two faults B + D/F
I (Mining) M2 Mb De-energized in firedamp One fault B + D/E/F
II (Surface) 1G Ga Zone 0 Two faults B + D/F
II (Surface) 1D Da Zone 20 Two faults B + D/F
II (Surface) 2G Gb Zone 1 (+ Zone 0) One fault B + D/E/F
II (Surface) 2D Db Zone 21 (+ Zone 20) One fault B + D/E/F
II (Surface) 3G Gc Zone 2 (+ Zone 1, 0) Normal operation A (self-cert)
II (Surface) 3D Dc Zone 22 (+ Zone 21, 20) Normal operation A (self-cert)

Important: Higher-category equipment can always be used in a lower-risk zone. Category 1 equipment is acceptable in Zones 0, 1, and 2. Category 2 equipment is acceptable in Zones 1 and 2, but not Zone 0. Category 3 equipment is only acceptable in Zone 2.

How Categories Appear on ATEX Markings

The equipment category is embedded in the ATEX nameplate marking. Here is how to decode it:

CE 0539
⚠ II 2 G Ex d IIC T4 Gb

Breaking this down:

  • II — Equipment Group II (surface industries)
  • 2 — Equipment Category 2 (suitable for Zone 1)
  • G — Gas atmosphere (G = gas, D = dust, GD = both)

Other marking examples:

ATEX Marking Meaning Suitable Zones
⚠ II 1 G Group II, Category 1, Gas Zone 0, 1, 2
⚠ II 1 D Group II, Category 1, Dust Zone 20, 21, 22
⚠ II 2 G Group II, Category 2, Gas Zone 1, 2
⚠ II 2 D Group II, Category 2, Dust Zone 21, 22
⚠ II 3 G Group II, Category 3, Gas Zone 2 only
⚠ II 3 GD Group II, Category 3, Gas and Dust Zone 2, 22
⚠ II 2(1) G Group II, primarily Cat 2, with Cat 1 components Zone 1, 2 (Cat 1 circuits in Zone 0)
⚠ I M1 Group I, Mining Category M1 Underground mines (stays energized)
⚠ I M2 Group I, Mining Category M2 Underground mines (de-energized in Ex atm)

Category vs EPL: What's the Difference?

Equipment categories (ATEX) and Equipment Protection Levels (IEC/IECEx) describe the same concept from different regulatory perspectives:

ATEX Category (Group II) IEC EPL (Gas) IEC EPL (Dust) Level of Protection
Category 1 Ga Da Very High — safe with 2 faults
Category 2 Gb Db High — safe with 1 fault
Category 3 Gc Dc Enhanced — safe in normal operation

Key distinction: ATEX categories are a regulatory concept — they determine the conformity assessment route and appear in the ATEX portion of the equipment marking. EPL is a technical concept from IEC 60079-0 — it appears in the technical portion of the marking (the Ga/Gb/Gc suffix). Both systems are used simultaneously on ATEX-certified equipment.

On a typical ATEX nameplate, you'll see both:

⚠ II 2 G  Ex d IIC T4 Gb
    ↑                    ↑
  ATEX Category 2      IEC EPL Gb

They always agree: Category 1 ↔ EPL "a", Category 2 ↔ EPL "b", Category 3 ↔ EPL "c".

Non-Electrical Equipment

ATEX categories also apply to non-electrical equipment — mechanical devices that could become ignition sources through hot surfaces, friction, sparks, or compression. Examples include:

  • Gearboxes and mechanical drives
  • Pumps and compressors
  • Fans and blowers
  • Conveyor systems
  • Manual tools (non-sparking)

Non-electrical equipment is assessed under EN 13463 series (or ISO 80079-36/37) rather than IEC 60079. The category system works identically — the category determines the zone suitability and conformity assessment route. Category 1 and 2 non-electrical equipment requires Notified Body involvement; Category 3 can be self-certified.

Conformity Assessment Modules

The ATEX Directive specifies different conformity assessment routes based on category:

Category Conformity Route Notified Body Role
Category 1 (M1) Module B (EU Type Examination) + Module D (QA of production) or Module F (Product verification) Design assessment + production surveillance
Category 2 (M2) — Electrical Module B + Module D, E, or F Design assessment; production module choice
Category 2 — Non-electrical Module B + Module D, E, or F (or Module B + Module C1 with NB involvement) Design assessment; production verification varies
Category 3 Module A (Internal production control) None — manufacturer self-certifies

Practical Selection Examples

Example 1: Junction Box in a Refinery

Scenario: Electrical junction box near a tank farm where hydrocarbon vapors may be present during valve operations (normal operation).

  • Zone classification: Zone 1 (explosive atmosphere likely during normal operation)
  • Gas group: IIA (propane/gasoline vapors)
  • Required category: Category 2G minimum
  • Typical selection: Ex e IIA T3 Gb junction box (increased safety)
  • ATEX marking: ⚠ II 2 G Ex e IIA T3 Gb

Example 2: LED Light in a Grain Silo

Scenario: Lighting fixture inside a grain storage silo where dust clouds form during filling operations.

  • Zone classification: Zone 21 (dust cloud likely during normal filling)
  • Dust type: Grain dust (non-conductive, Group IIIB)
  • Required category: Category 2D minimum
  • Typical selection: Ex tb IIIB T125°C Db LED luminaire
  • ATEX marking: ⚠ II 2 D Ex tb IIIB T125°C Db

Example 3: Temperature Transmitter in a Hydrogen Electrolyser

Scenario: Temperature measurement instrument inside the electrolyser enclosure where hydrogen is always present.

  • Zone classification: Zone 0 (hydrogen continuously present)
  • Gas group: IIC (hydrogen)
  • Required category: Category 1G
  • Typical selection: Ex ia IIC T4 Ga temperature transmitter (intrinsically safe)
  • ATEX marking: ⚠ II 1 G Ex ia IIC T4 Ga

Example 4: Motor in a Paint Spray Booth Ventilation Area

Scenario: Extraction fan motor in the ventilation duct downstream of a paint spray booth. Solvent vapors only present under abnormal conditions.

  • Zone classification: Zone 2 (explosive atmosphere not expected during normal operation)
  • Gas group: IIA (typical paint solvents)
  • Required category: Category 3G minimum
  • Typical selection: Ex nA IIA T3 Gc motor
  • ATEX marking: ⚠ II 3 G Ex nA IIA T3 Gc
  • Cost benefit: Category 3 motor costs ~€800 vs ~€2,500 for an equivalent Category 2 motor

Example 5: Mining Communication Radio

Scenario: Portable two-way radio for use by miners in an underground coal mine with methane risk.

  • Environment: Underground mine (Group I)
  • Required category: M1 (must remain energized during methane event for safety communications)
  • Typical selection: Ex ia I Ma portable radio
  • ATEX marking: ⚠ I M1 Ex ia I Ma

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing Category number with gas group: Category 1/2/3 describes protection level and zone suitability. Gas group IIA/IIB/IIC describes which gases the equipment is certified for. They are independent parameters.
  • Using Category 3 equipment in Zone 1: This is a compliance violation. Category 3 (EPL Gc/Dc) equipment provides no fault protection and is only suitable for Zone 2/22.
  • Assuming Category 2 covers Zone 0: Category 2 equipment is designed for one-fault tolerance. Zone 0 requires two-fault tolerance (Category 1). Only Ex ia and Ex ma protection methods qualify for Zone 0.
  • Ignoring the gas/dust suffix: A product marked II 2 G is certified for gas only. Using it in a dust environment (which requires D suffix) is not permitted. Equipment for both environments must be marked GD.
  • Over-specifying category: Installing Category 1 equipment throughout a Zone 2 facility is technically compliant but economically wasteful. Proper zone classification and category matching optimizes the cost of an installation. See the equipment selection guide.

Relationship to Other Standards Systems

ATEX equipment categories are specific to the EU/EEA regulatory framework. Other systems handle the same concept differently:

  • IECEx: Uses EPL (Ga/Gb/Gc, Da/Db/Dc) without the ATEX category system. Equipment is certified to IEC 60079 standards with EPL marking.
  • NEC (North America): Uses Class/Division or Zone system. No direct "category" equivalent — equipment is listed for specific Division or Zone use. See our NEC vs ATEX/IECEx comparison.
  • UK (post-Brexit): UKCA marking retains the same category structure as ATEX. DSEAR workplace requirements reference categories identically.
